The Adversary Has Accelerated.  
Has Your Security Strategy Kept Pace?  
What the CrowdStrike 2026 Global Threat Report really means for AI security leaders

Twenty-seven seconds.  
This is the fastest recorded eCrime breakout time in 2025: the interval between an adversary gaining initial access and moving laterally within the network. Twenty-seven seconds is shorter than the time it takes to read a typical notification. The CrowdStrike 2026 Global Threat Report makes it clear: the threat landscape did not simply evolve in 2025. It accelerated, became more elusive, and, in a significant shift, began leveraging organisations’ own AI tools against them.  
If you approach this report as a routine technical update, you risk missing its broader implications.  
This report represents a strategic inflection point.  
Critical Numbers for Security Leaders  
Security leaders are accustomed to reviewing statistics and moving on. However, the 2026 report requires a deeper consideration of these figures and their operational impact.  
• 89% increase in attacks by AI-enabled adversaries year-over-year, representing a near doubling of AI-driven threat activity. • 29-minute average eCrime breakout time, a 65% increase in speed from 2024. In one case, data exfiltration began within four minutes of initial access.

• 82% of detections in 2025 were malware-free, with adversaries using valid credentials, trusted identity flows, and approved SaaS integrations. • 42% of vulnerabilities were exploited before public disclosure, outpacing most organisations’ patch cycles. • 266% increase in cloud intrusions from state-nexus actors, making the cloud the primary risk environment.  
These figures illustrate an adversary that is faster, more discreet, and better equipped than those for which most organisations have prepared.  
"AI is compressing the time between intent and execution while turning enterprise AI systems into targets. Security teams must operate faster than the adversary to win."  
— Adam Meyers, Head of Counter Adversary Operations, CrowdStrike

Key Insight: AI Is Both Weapon and Target  
The report’s most significant finding for AI security leaders is not related to speed  
or volume, but rather to surface area.  
Adversaries exploited legitimate GenAI tools at more than 90 organisations in  
2025 — injecting malicious prompts to generate commands for credential theft  
and data exfiltration. They abused AI development platforms to establish  
persistence and deploy ransomware. They published malicious AI servers  
impersonating trusted services to intercept sensitive data.  
This shift must be a top priority for AI security leaders. Tools adopted to increase efficiency have become attack vectors. The prompt interface is now an entry point, the AI agent a pivot point, and the development environment a persistent mechanism.  
Prompts now function as malware, yet most organisations lack  
visibility into this layer.  
Evasion Is an Architectural Challenge

The report identifies 2025 as the year of the evasive adversary, and this evasion is not  
primarily technical but structural.  
Adversaries exploit trusted relationships such as supply chains, SaaS integrations, valid credentials, and inherited software ecosystems. They blend into normal activity, making the distinction between legitimate and malicious actions increasingly unclear in cloud-native, AI-integrated environments.  
Chinese-nexus actors exploited edge devices in 40% of their intrusions, targeting systems with minimal EDR coverage and limited defender visibility. North Korea’s most advanced adversary group conducted a $1.46 billion cryptocurrency theft using trojanized software delivered through a supply-chain compromise. These are not merely technical exploits; they are breaches of trust.  
The architectural implications are significant. Perimeter-based and endpoint-centric security models were designed for different threats. With 82% of detections involving no malware and intrusions leveraging approved tools and legitimate credentials, the challenge now lies in visibility and identity management.  
What This Means in Practice: Three Reorientations  
The report provides intelligence; how AI security leaders respond determines strategy. The following areas require a shift in approach:  
1. Secure the AI layer before it becomes a liability. If your organisation uses GenAI tools, chat interfaces, or agentic workflows and lacks visibility into the prompt and interaction layer, this is a critical blind spot. Adversaries are already exploiting this. Real-time AI security is now an immediate requirement, not a future consideration.  
2. Treat identity as the primary control plane. With most intrusions involving valid credentials, identity management is now the front line of defence. Non-human identities, AI agent identities, and service account hygiene require the same level of scrutiny as privileged human access. Standing privileges represent a significant vulnerability.  
3. Reduce detection-to-response time. With a 29-minute average breakout time, traditional human-speed SOC operations are insufficient for initial triage. This is not a critique of human judgment, but a call to focus human expertise where it is most impactful.

Automation should address the initial response, while analysts focus on  
decisions that require context and judgment.  
The Fundamental Question Raised by the Report  
Each year, global threat reports are reviewed and shared, but organisations often continue to operate with outdated threat models. While data improves, security architectures frequently remain unchanged.  
The CrowdStrike 2026 report poses a critical question: Is your security operating at the speed of today’s adversaries? Do not settle for awareness alone. Use intelligence to drive architectural change, not just inform documentation.

A QUESTION FOR AI SECURITY LEADERS  
If your organisation experienced an intrusion today, what is your actual detection-to-containment time? Can you respond within 29 minutes? Assess and adapt your defences now rather than waiting for the next threat report.
